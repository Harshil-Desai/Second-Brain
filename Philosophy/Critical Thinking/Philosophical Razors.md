# Philosophical Razors

A **razor** is a rule or principle for determining explanations by *cutting away* the less likely ones.

## Occam's Razor

> The simplest explanation, requiring the fewest assumptions, is the most likely to be correct.

It cuts through clutter and confusion. It's not always correct, but the more assumptions you have to make, the more you complicate something — which makes it less likely to be true compared to simpler alternatives.

## Sagan Standard

> Extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence.

How do you accept a claim that doesn't have proper evidence behind it? You don't.

## Hitchens's Razor

> What can be asserted without evidence can also be dismissed without evidence.

The burden of proof is always on the person making the claim. If they don't prove it, there's no productive discussion to be had.

## Alder's Razor

> What cannot be settled by experiment or observation is not worth debating.

For example: if we think an event was "too absurd to occur," that's really just our expectations being undermined. If a cup started floating in the air, would that be absurd? Not really — we'd just need to observe all the variables involved. If we can't find or observe them, the argument can never settle, so it's not worth debating.
